Chapter 07

Export & Import Regulations

Sections 7 & 8 of FEMA deal with export and import of goods & services. The core mandate: every exporter must bring forex back to India, and every importer must ensure they actually receive what they paid for.

📦
Why FEMA cares about trade: India's forex reserves come primarily from trade earnings. If exporters don't repatriate their earnings, the country loses precious forex. And if importers over-invoice or make payments without receiving goods, it's a potential channel for illegal capital flight. FEMA's trade provisions exist to protect India's forex integrity.

Export of Goods — The Complete Flow

Section 7 mandates that every exporter must receive and repatriate the full export value. Here's how it works:

1
Export Order

Exporter receives order from foreign buyer

2
GR/SDF Form

File declaration with AD bank (form GR for physical exports)

3
Customs Clearance

Goods cleared by customs; shipping bill filed via ICEGATE

4
Ship/Dispatch

Goods leave India

5
Realize Payment

Receive forex from buyer (LC/TT/DP/DA)

6
Repatriate

Bring forex to India within 9 months

📋 Key Export Compliance Points

  • Declaration mandatory: Every export must be declared on GR/SDF/PP/SOFTEX form
  • Repatriation deadline: Full export value must be received within 9 months from date of export (for goods); extendable by AD bank
  • For services: SOFTEX form for software exports; other services follow general current account norms
  • Export proceeds: Must be in freely convertible currency or Indian Rupees (for ACU/bilateral arrangements)
  • Write-off: AD bank can write off unrealized export bills up to 5% of total export at discretion; beyond needs RBI
  • Advance payment: Must be adjusted against future exports or refunded within timelines

⚠️ Export Don'ts

  • ❌ Never export without filing declaration to AD/customs
  • ❌ Never give commission to agents exceeding prescribed limits
  • ❌ Never under-invoice exports (declaring lower value than actual)
  • ❌ Never delay repatriation beyond 9 months without extension
  • ❌ Never receive payment in non-convertible currencies without permission
  • ❌ Never accept payment in modes other than normal banking channels (cash, crypto etc.)

Import of Goods — The Complete Flow

1
Import Order

Place order with foreign supplier

2
Open LC / Remit

AD bank opens Letter of Credit or makes advance payment

3
Goods Arrive

Goods reach Indian port/airport

4
Customs Clearance

File Bill of Entry, pay customs duty, clear goods

5
Payment

Settle payment through AD bank within 6 months

6
Evidence

Submit Bill of Entry as evidence of import

📋 Key Import Rules

  • Evidence required: AD bank must obtain Bill of Entry or customs document as evidence that goods were imported
  • Payment timeline: Import payment within 6 months from date of shipment (extendable)
  • Advance payment: Allowed with conditions (security like bank guarantee for large amounts)
  • Imports for personal use: Generally free, subject to customs duty
  • Import against forex earned: EEFCs (Exchange Earners Foreign Currency) accounts can be used to pay for imports
  • Gold/silver import: Special rules — only nominated agencies/banks can import

💳 Payment Methods

  • Letter of Credit (LC): Most secure; bank guarantees payment
  • Documents against Payment (DP): Pay on receipt of documents
  • Documents against Acceptance (DA): Accept bill of exchange, pay later
  • Open Account / TT: Direct wire transfer (riskier for seller)
  • Advance payment: Pay before goods shipped
  • Buyer's Credit: Foreign bank finances the import payment

Export of Services — The IT Industry Backbone

India's IT services exports are a massive forex earner (USD 190+ billion annually). SOFTEX form is the key compliance document.

  • SOFTEX Form: Filed with STPI/SEZ for software exports. Contains contract details, invoice value, customer info.
  • Realization: Same 9-month rule applies for services. But services often have ongoing contracts, so AD banks manage the tracking.
  • Mode 1-4 of service export: Cross-border supply (Mode 1, most IT exports), consumption abroad (Mode 2), commercial presence (Mode 3), movement of natural persons (Mode 4).
  • BPO/KPO: Back-office services, knowledge process outsourcing — all classified as service exports. Massive contributors to India's current account balance.

🚨 Consequences of Non-Compliance

Non-realization of export proceeds or irregular import payments can lead to:

  • Action by Enforcement Directorate under Section 13 — penalty up to 3× the amount involved
  • Additional daily penalty of ₹5,000 for continuing contravention
  • Debarment from future trade — AD banks may refuse to process your transactions
  • RBI's EDPMS/IDPMS system flags overdue export/import bills automatically
  • Company directors can be held personally liable if default is attributable to their negligence
