Chapter 13

FEMA vs FERA — The Paradigm Shift

Understanding the shift from FERA (1973) to FEMA (1999) is crucial — it represents India's transformation from a closed, suspicious, control-oriented economy to an open, facilitative, market-friendly one.

🔒 FERA 1973

Foreign Exchange Regulation Act

Born in an era of forex scarcity (India had barely 2 weeks of import cover). Every rupee of forex was precious. The mindset: "Foreign exchange is a scarce national resource — conserve it, control it, restrict it."

Philosophy: Everything is prohibited unless specifically permitted.

✅ FEMA 1999

Foreign Exchange Management Act

Born after 1991 liberalization, when India started building forex reserves and integrating with the global economy. The mindset: "Facilitate trade and payments, promote orderly development of the forex market."

Philosophy: Everything is permitted unless specifically regulated.

Detailed Feature Comparison

ParameterFERA 1973FEMA 1999
Full Name Foreign Exchange Regulation Act Foreign Exchange Management Act
Year 1973 (effective 1974) 1999 (effective 1 June 2000)
Keyword "Regulation" — control and restrict "Management" — facilitate and manage
Approach Everything prohibited unless permitted (positive list) Current account: free unless restricted; Capital account: permitted based on regulations
Nature of Offence Criminal — violation is a criminal offence Civil — violation is a civil contravention
Punishment Imprisonment up to 7 years + unlimited fine Monetary penalty up to 3× amount involved; No imprisonment
Burden of Proof On the accused (reverse burden — "guilty until proven innocent") On the prosecution (normal civil standard — "preponderance of probability")
Arrest Powers ED could arrest without warrant; detained persons treated as criminal accused No arrest powers for FEMA contraventions (but PMLA arrest powers exist if ML suspected)
Bail Non-bailable in many cases; stringent bail conditions Not applicable — no imprisonment, so no bail question
Current Account Heavily restricted; RBI approval needed for most transactions Free by default; only Schedule I-III exceptions
Capital Account Almost completely prohibited for residents Progressively liberalized; LRS allows USD 250,000/year for individuals
FDI Severely restricted; most sectors prohibited; 40% cap in most cases Most sectors 100% open under automatic route
Adjudication Criminal courts (Magistrate/Sessions court) Quasi-judicial Adjudicating Authority → Special Director → Appellate Tribunal → HC
Compounding Not available (criminal offences can't be "compounded") Section 15 allows voluntary compounding — settle and move on
Sections 81 Sections 49 Sections (much simpler)
Applicability Applied to Indian citizens (citizenship-based approach) Applied to persons based on residential status (residence-based approach)
Forex Reserves Context India had ~USD 1 billion (barely 2 weeks of import cover) India has USD 600+ billion (10+ months of import cover)

Impact of the FERA → FEMA Transition

📈

FDI Explosion

FDI inflows went from ~USD 2 billion/year (1990s) to USD 80+ billion/year (2020s) after FEMA's liberalization

💱

Forex Reserves

From USD 1 billion (1991 crisis) to USD 600+ billion today — one of the world's largest

🧑‍💻

IT Services Boom

FEMA's facilitative framework enabled India's IT services industry to grow to a USD 190+ billion export powerhouse

🤝

Business Confidence

Decriminalization of forex violations removed the "fear factor" — businesses could operate without threat of arrest for technical violations

🌍

Global Integration

Indians can now invest abroad (LRS), NRIs invest in India freely, MNCs set up easily — all impossible under FERA

⚖️

Rule of Law

Quasi-judicial proceedings with natural justice principles replaced arbitrary criminal proceedings of FERA era

📖
The famous George Fernandes story: Under FERA, even Union Ministers were prosecuted for minor forex violations. Defence Minister George Fernandes was charged under FERA for accepting $5,000 from a foreign organization for a lecture. Under FEMA, this would have been a simple current account transaction — perfectly legal without any approval. This anecdote illustrates how draconian FERA was.
