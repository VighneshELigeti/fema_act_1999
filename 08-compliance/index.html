Chapter 08

Compliance Framework

FEMA compliance is an ongoing obligation — it's not just about getting the initial transaction right, but about filing returns, maintaining records, and ensuring continuous adherence to regulations.

Three Pillars of FEMA Compliance

📝

Reporting & Filing

Every significant forex transaction must be reported to RBI through AD banks using prescribed forms and within prescribed timelines. Late or non-filings attract penalties.

📂

Record Keeping

All documents related to forex transactions must be maintained for a minimum of 5 years. This includes contracts, invoices, bank certificates, forms, approvals, and correspondence.

✅

Ongoing Conditions

Many FEMA permissions come with conditions (pricing guidelines, sectoral caps, end-use restrictions). Compliance means not just the initial transaction but ongoing adherence to all conditions.

Annual FEMA Compliance Calendar

April–June

Begin FY Compliance

Review outstanding EDPMS/IDPMS caution listings. Plan LRS utilization for the year. Check any pending FC-GPR/FC-TRS filings from prior year.

July 15

FLA Return Due

All Indian companies that have received FDI and/or have overseas investments must file Foreign Liabilities and Assets Return with RBI. This is a census survey — mandatory, not optional.

September 30

Annual Return on Foreign Assets (ARFA)

Under Income Tax (Black Money Act) — declare foreign assets. Overlaps with FEMA reporting requirements. Cross-reference your FEMA filings.

December 31

APR Due for ODI

Annual Performance Report for overseas JVs/WOS must be filed with RBI by Dec 31 for the preceding March-ended FY. Includes financial statements of the overseas entity.

Monthly

ECB-2 Returns

If you have active ECBs, monthly ECB-2 returns must be filed within 7 days of month-end. Covers drawdown, utilization, outstanding balance, repayment details.

Ongoing

EDPMS/IDPMS Monitoring

RBI's electronic platforms (Export Data Processing and Monitoring System / Import Data Processing and Monitoring System) track every export/import transaction. Outstanding entries get flagged and may trigger ED action.

Compounding — The "Settlement" Route

🤝
What is compounding? Compounding under Section 15 is FEMA's "settlement" mechanism. If you've committed a FEMA contravention, instead of going through the full adjudication process (which can take years), you can apply for compounding — admit the violation, pay a compounding fee, and close the matter. It's like paying a fine to settle. RBI compounds most contraventions (up to ₹5 crore penalty); ED compounds the rest.

When you discover a FEMA contravention (through internal audit, external compliance review, or notice from RBI/ED). Early compounding = lower penalty, faster closure. It's voluntary — you don't have to wait for ED to come knocking. Many companies proactively compound technical violations found during due diligence (M&A, IPO prep).

RBI's Compounding Guidelines specify a matrix based on: (1) Amount involved, (2) Duration of contravention, (3) Nature of violation (technical vs deliberate), (4) Whether voluntary or detected. Typical fees range from ₹10,000 to several lakhs. The fee is NOT the same as the penalty under Section 13 — compounding fees are generally much lower than adjudication penalties.

Repeat violations (second or subsequent contravention of the same nature within 3 years), contraventions involving money laundering suspicion, violations where ED has already initiated adjudication proceedings. Also, some specific violations may be excluded from compounding per RBI circular.
