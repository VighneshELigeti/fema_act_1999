Chapter 01

Objectives & Approach

Understanding why India shifted from a restrictive forex regime to a facilitative one — and what FEMA aims to achieve.

The Big Picture

Imagine you're running a country that just became independent in 1947. Foreign exchange (forex) is extremely scarce. Every dollar, pound, and yen coming in or going out needs to be carefully controlled. You can't afford to let people freely move money across borders — your economy depends on conserving every bit of forex.

That's exactly the mindset behind FERA (Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1973). It was built on fear and scarcity. The philosophy was: "Foreign exchange is scarce. We must conserve it. Anyone who violates the rules is a criminal."

Fast-forward to 1991. India opens its economy. Foreign investment starts pouring in. India's forex reserves grow from almost zero to billions of dollars. Suddenly, the old "scarcity" mindset doesn't make sense anymore. The world has changed, and India needs a law that facilitates forex transactions, not one that criminalizes them.

🔑
The Core Shift: FEMA moved India from a regime of "conservation" of foreign exchange to one of "management" — from strict control to smart regulation.

Two Philosophies: Restriction vs. Facilitation

❌ FERA Approach (Old)

"Everything is prohibited unless explicitly permitted."

  • Forex transactions = criminal offences
  • Guilty until proven innocent
  • Punishment: imprisonment (up to 7 years)
  • ED had arbitrary arrest powers
  • Burden of proof on the accused
  • Mindset: forex is scarce, hoard it

✓ FEMA Approach (New)

"Everything is permitted unless specifically prohibited or regulated."

  • Forex violations = civil contraventions
  • Innocent until proven guilty
  • Penalty: monetary fines (up to 3× amount)
  • No arbitrary arrests
  • Burden of proof on the department
  • Mindset: facilitate trade, manage risks

Core Objectives of FEMA

The Preamble of FEMA itself declares its purpose. Let's break it down:

"An Act to consolidate and amend the law relating to foreign exchange with the objective of facilitating external trade and payments and for promoting the orderly development and maintenance of foreign exchange market in India."
🌐

Facilitate External Trade

Make it easy for Indian businesses to import materials, export goods, and engage in international commerce without unnecessary bureaucratic hurdles.

💳

Facilitate Payments

Enable smooth cross-border payments — for services, royalties, dividends, travel, education, medical treatment, and more.

📊

Orderly Forex Market

Develop a well-functioning forex market where the rupee's value is determined by market forces (within managed boundaries).

🛡️

Maintain Stability

Prevent disorderly conditions — like sudden capital flight or speculative attacks — that could destabilize the rupee and the economy.

🔄

Current Account Convertibility

India committed to full current account convertibility under IMF Article VIII. FEMA implements this — all legitimate current account transactions are freely permitted.

📈

Progressive Capital Account Liberalization

Gradually open capital account transactions (investments, borrowings) as the economy matures and forex reserves grow — but maintain safeguards.

The Balancing Act

FEMA's genius lies in its balance. It doesn't throw the doors wide open (that could cause instability), nor does it keep them shut (that would stifle growth). Instead, it sets up a system of managed openness:

Key Approach Principles

For current account transactions (day-to-day cross-border payments like imports, travel, education), the default is "permitted". You don't need special permission to send money abroad for legitimate current account purposes. Only specific prohibitions/restrictions are listed.

Think of it like: "The door is open. We've only put up a few 'No Entry' signs on specific paths."

For capital account transactions (investments, borrowings, property), the approach is more cautious. These are permitted only as specified by RBI/Government. But the range of what's permitted has been steadily expanding over the years.

"The door is partially open. We control how wide it opens, and we're gradually opening it wider."

All forex transactions must go through authorized persons (mostly banks licensed by RBI). This ensures traceability, compliance, and that proper KYC/AML checks are done.

This is the "plumbing" of FEMA — even if a transaction is permitted, it must flow through the right pipes.

Unlike FERA which could put you in jail, FEMA treats violations as civil wrongs. The punishment is monetary — penalty up to 3 times the sum involved. This doesn't mean violations are taken lightly, but it removes the stigma and harshness of criminal prosecution for forex missteps.

⚠️
Exception: If a FEMA contravention also involves money laundering (PMLA), the consequences become much more serious — including criminal prosecution. FEMA itself is civil; the intersection with PMLA can bring criminal consequences.

The Reserve Bank of India is the day-to-day administrator of FEMA. It issues regulations (called "FEMA Regulations"), circulars, master directions, and processes applications. The Central Government makes broader policy decisions (like FDI policy, current account restriction rules), but RBI handles the operational machinery.

FDI Allowed in Many Sectors

The shift from FERA to FEMA has been transformative. India went from a forex-starved economy to one of the world's largest holders of foreign exchange reserves. Companies like Coca-Cola (which had left India under FERA in 1977) came back. FDI inflows surged. Indians can now freely invest abroad, travel, pay for education overseas, and buy foreign goods — things that were heavily restricted or impossible under FERA.
