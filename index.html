Overview

What is FEMA?

FEMA replaced the older, restrictive FERA regime in 1999 to facilitate external trade and promote orderly development of India's forex market.

💡
Key Shift: FEMA treats most forex violations as civil contraventions (with monetary penalties), unlike FERA which treated them as criminal offences (with imprisonment). This was a fundamental philosophy change from "regulate and restrict" to "facilitate and manage."

Historical Timeline

1947
Foreign Exchange Regulation Act (FERA)
India's first comprehensive forex law. Very restrictive — treated most violations as criminal offences. Forex was scarce post-independence.
1973
FERA Strengthened
Major amendments made FERA even more draconian. Companies like Coca-Cola and IBM left India rather than comply with harsh regulations.
1991
Economic Liberalization
Balance of Payments crisis → LPG reforms. India opened its economy, making strict FERA outdated and counter-productive.
1993
FERA Amended
Partial relaxation of FERA provisions to align with liberalization. But the fundamental approach was still restrictive.
1997
Tarapore Committee
Recommended capital account convertibility and a modern forex management framework, paving the way for FEMA.
1999
FEMA Enacted
Parliament passes the Foreign Exchange Management Act (Act No. 42 of 1999). A paradigm shift from conservation to management of forex.
June 1, 2000
FEMA Comes into Force
FEMA officially replaces FERA. All forex transactions in India now governed under the new facilitative regime.
2004
LRS Introduced
Liberalized Remittance Scheme allows residents to freely remit up to $25,000 (later raised to $250,000) per year.
2015–2019
Major Rule Overhauls
New FDI rules, ODI regulations, ECB norms revised. FEMA regulations continuously updated by RBI to keep pace with global trends.
2022–2024
Overseas Investment Rules & Digital
Comprehensive new Overseas Investment Rules 2022. TCS on LRS. Digital forex and fintech considerations evolve.

Structure of the Act

FEMA has 49 sections organized into 7 chapters, supported by numerous Rules (by Central Government) and Regulations/Directions (by RBI).

Contains the short title, extent, commencement, and definitions. Section 2 alone has 28+ definitions that form the foundation of the entire regulatory framework.

The heart of the Act. Section 3 prohibits dealing in forex except through authorized persons. Sections 4 & 5 govern current and capital account transactions. Sections 6–9 cover export/import of currency, realization of foreign exchange, and exemptions.

Who can deal in forex? Only authorized persons (AD Category I, II, III banks, Full Fledged Money Changers, etc.) licensed by the RBI. Covers licensing, revocation, duties, and obligations.

Civil contravention framework. Penalty up to 3× the sum involved or ₹2 lakh (whichever is more). Continuing default: up to ₹5,000 per day. Power to compound contraventions.

The largest chapter. Covers: Adjudicating Authority appointment and powers, appeal to Special Director (Appeals), Appellate Tribunal composition and procedure, appeal to High Court, enforcement of orders, and recovery of penalties.

Empowers the Directorate of Enforcement (ED) to investigate contraventions, search premises, seize documents, and arrest in extraordinary cases. The ED is the enforcement arm of FEMA.

Power to make rules/regulations, exemptions, application of other laws, bar of legal proceedings, protection of officers acting in good faith, and transitional provisions from FERA to FEMA.
